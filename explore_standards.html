The Digital Literacy Standards have been helpfully grouped into four categories:

  1. Impossible to Teach / Internally Contradictory
  2. Possible to Teach, but undesirable
  3. Valuable to students, but only because their lives will be easier if they embrace techno-feudalism
  4. Valuable to students

Return Home

Impossible to teach

  • Manage digital wellbeing: Cannot be taught because it doesn't exist
  • Manage online privacy: Cannot be taught, as online privacy is impossible

 

Possible to teach, but undesirable

  • Manage digital identity: Fraudulant concept, digital identity is just your regular identity but monetisable
  • Collaborate and exchange: Key element in the training of digital serfs.
  • Locate information: Curation is the enemy of creativity.
  • Collect and collate data: part of AI indoctrination, and data is available because someone has made it available as part of their propaganda efforts.
  • Plan and Design: dehumanises the process of design. Project management is really boring and they can learn MS project later in life.
  • Create Content: Second key component of serf-training
  • Respect Intellectual Property: Pure propaganda
  • Manage Content: i.e. start paying those cloud subscriptions

 

Valuable to students, but only because their lives will be easier if they embrace techno-feudalism

  • Communicate: It's probably better to embrace deeply unsatisfying forms of communication than be even more alone
  • Protect Content: The reference to firewalls and anti-malware applications is a little dated and reflects the pre-web 2.0 early 2000s internet optimism of ACARA's approach more broadly. But the year level goals are solid.

Valuable to students

  • Interpret data: Visualising things is always fun
  • Select and Operate Tools: If digital literacy was regular literacy, this one would be "learn to read and write". The rest of the standards are either superfluous or actively harmful.
